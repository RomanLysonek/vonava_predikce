01 / The business behind the rows

Beauty retail is a promise made before demand arrives

A customer sees a product, price and offer now; the commercial and fulfilment system must already be ready.

Notino is an online-first European beauty retailer: perfumes, skincare, make-up, hair care and personal-care products from many brands, complemented by stores and pickup points, private-label products, marketplace activity and value-added services. That makes demand forecasting a commercial capability, not just a statistics exercise.

AssortmentMany products, brands, price points and replenishment profiles compete for attention and working capital.
ChannelsWeb, app and physical touchpoints shape when, where and under which incentive a purchase happens.
Retail rhythmWeekdays, gifting seasons, campaigns and product availability create synchronized demand shocks.
Profit engineRetail margin, private-label economics, marketplace commissions and services reward different decisions.

Public business context is intentionally kept general; the quantitative claims below come from the supplied assignment data.

03 / How we modeled it

Start with a retail memory, then learn its corrections

The final system is easier to understand as five decisions than as a catalogue of algorithms.

1

Repair the calendar

Separate pre-launch periods, missing observations, unavailable days and genuine zero sales instead of replacing all absence with zero.

2

Anchor in weekly behaviour

Use recent same-weekday demand as a strong baseline, because a Monday resembles prior Mondays more than an arbitrary nearby date.

3

Add known context

Give the model calendar, price, campaign, discount, lifecycle, market and event features that would actually be known for each target date.

4

Predict seven days directly

Build one row per future horizon from the same observed origin. Later days do not consume earlier predictions, avoiding compounding forecast error.

5

Validate as history unfolded

Repeat seven-day forecasts at historical cutoffs and score common available-product rows. WAPE leads selection; bias and coverage guard the result.

04 / The missing bridge: changeable features

A forecast answers "what will sell?" - not yet "what should we do?"

Price, discount and campaign fields were used as future-known inputs. We did not estimate their causal effects or optimize them.

Important boundary: changing a promo value in the current model creates a model-based scenario, not a reliable incremental-sales claim. Historical promos were targeted by the business, so correlation mixes the offer effect with seasonality, product choice, customer intent and other selection effects.
1 / Build treatment-quality dataAdd list price, net selling price, unit cost, promo mechanics, placement, traffic, inventory quantity, competitor price and customer/market context. Record why and when an offer was assigned.
2 / Estimate elasticity and upliftUse randomized holdouts where possible; otherwise apply causal panels, difference-in-differences, doubly robust learners or hierarchical elasticity models with product/category pooling.
3 / Separate demand from fulfilmentEstimate lost sales from inventory trajectories, page views, substitutions and replenishment events. A profitable optimizer needs latent demand, not only censored sales.
4 / Create a guarded simulatorExpose price/promo scenarios only inside historically supported ranges, return uncertainty bands, and flag extrapolation rather than presenting one precise but fragile number.
5 / Optimize the decisionChoose price, discount, channel and campaign under margin, stock, brand, fairness, legal and operational constraints - then measure incrementality with controlled experiments.
05 / From accuracy to profit

The best commercial action balances volume, margin and risk

Maximizing units can destroy margin; maximizing price can destroy conversion. The objective must value the full outcome.

Expected contribution = (price - unit cost - variable fulfilment cost) x expected units - promo cost - stockout/overstock risk
Forecast

Know the likely baseline

Predict demand and uncertainty under the currently planned offer, by product, date and channel.

Simulate

Compare credible actions

Estimate incremental units and substitution for candidate prices, promos, placements and stock allocations.

Optimize

Select constrained profit

Maximize expected contribution while respecting inventory, brand rules, service level and operational capacity.

Learn

Close the causal loop

Run holdouts, measure realized incrementality and feed results back into elasticity and decision models.

06 / How I would help as a new colleague

Turn a credible forecast into a trusted decision product

The first contribution is not another complex model. It is connecting data, commercial assumptions and measured outcomes.

Listen and map

Work with pricing, category, CRM, supply and finance teams to document which decisions are changeable, when they are made and which constraints are real.

Make economics explicit

Join unit cost, net revenue, promo funding, returns and fulfilment cost so model evaluation reflects contribution, not accuracy alone.

Earn causal evidence

Design small holdouts and natural-experiment analyses, beginning with high-volume products where uncertainty has meaningful financial value.

Ship safely

Build monitored scenarios and recommendations with uncertainty, approvals, rollback rules and clear forecast-versus-realized diagnostics.

The destination is a learning commercial system: forecast what customers are likely to buy, understand which actions genuinely change that demand, choose the action with the best risk-adjusted contribution, and learn from every result.
