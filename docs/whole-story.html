01 / The business behind the rows

Beauty retail is a promise made before demand arrives

A customer sees an offer now. Stock, pricing and fulfilment have to be ready before the order arrives.

Notino is an online-first European beauty retailer selling perfumes, skincare, make-up and hair care.

Many choicesDemand is spread across products, brands and price points.
More than one channelThe same customer may move between web, app and stores.
A strong rhythmWeekdays, gifting seasons and campaigns can move many products at once.
Limited stockToo little loses sales; too much ties up cash and invites discounting.

03 / How we modeled it

Start with what usually happens, then adjust

The model follows a simple line of thought.

1

Clean the history

A missing row, a stockout and a genuine zero are not the same thing.

2

Remember the weekday

A Monday usually tells us more about the next Monday than an arbitrary nearby day.

3

Read the situation

Price, campaign, calendar and retail events explain why this week may differ.

4

Predict the week

All seven days start from the same known history, so one bad prediction cannot spoil the next.

5

Replay the past

We tested the process at old cut-off dates before trusting it on the real week.

04 / The missing bridge: changeable features

The forecast sees a promo. It does not yet know what the promo caused.

We used planned prices and campaigns to predict the week ahead. We did not use the model to choose them.

The catch: promos are not handed out at random. They often appear on products, dates or seasons that were already expected to behave differently. That pollutes the historical view: we see sales with the promo, but not what the same day would have looked like without it.
First, keep better contextRecord the offer, placement, traffic, stock and reason for running the promo - not only the final discount.
Then, create a fair comparisonSmall holdout groups can show what changed because of the promo rather than what happened beside it.
Finally, test useful choicesCompare a few realistic price and promo options, including their margin and stock impact, before recommending one.
05 / From accuracy to profit

More sales do not automatically mean more profit

A deep discount may lift volume and still leave the business worse off. A high price can protect margin and lose the customer.

Expected contribution = (price - unit cost - variable fulfilment cost) x expected units - promo cost - stockout/overstock risk

06 / How I would help as a new colleague

My first job would be to connect the model to real decisions

Not by adding complexity first, but by understanding how people use the answer.

Listen first

Learn when pricing, stock and campaign decisions are made - and what can actually change.

Add the economics

Bring cost and margin into the same view as forecast accuracy.

Test small

Use focused experiments to learn which offers truly add sales.

Make it usable

Show clear options, uncertainty and what happened after the decision.

The goal is simple: know what is likely to sell, learn what we can genuinely influence, and make the next decision more profitable than the last.
